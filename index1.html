NeurIPS 2025 Workshop

Biosecurity Safeguards for Generative AI

San Diego, California, USA





Motivation

Generative AI is revolutionizing biotechnology with immense potential in drug discovery, protein engineering, and synthetic biology, but this progress brings profound biosecurity concerns. GenAI's capacity to design novel biomolecules that could bypass existing safety screens, or be misused to engineer pathogens and toxins, presents credible dual-use threats, further amplified by AI-driven automation and the potential for AI science agents to accelerate experimental designs (Baker & Church, 2024; Bloomfield et al., 2024; Blau et al., 2024; Wittmann et al., 2024; Office of Science and Technology Policy, 2024; Boiko et al., 2023).


While biosecurity dangers in biotechnology have been recognized for over two decades, AI significantly accelerates and amplifies these risks, a concern echoed by leading scientists and even developers of powerful genome and protein foundation models (Church, 2004; Nguyen et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024). Despite growing attention to general AI safety (Bengio et al., 2025), the distinct and critical field of AI biosecurity remains dangerously underexplored. This gap is particularly alarming given the inherent dual-use nature of biotechnology, the high stakes of biological manipulation, the complex domain-specific knowledge required, and a pervasive shortage of cross-disciplinary expertise bridging AI safety and the biosciences.


Currently, many AI advancements in biotechnology are deployed without robust, built-in protections, exposing significant vulnerabilities and creating an urgent imperative for action. In a recent Nature Biotechnology article, "A call for built-in biosecurity safeguards for generative AI tools," (Wang et al., 2025) highlighted these escalating risks and advocates for the proactive development and integration of AI-native safeguards embedded within GenAI systems for the life sciences. This workshop is directly motivated by this critical call and the urgent need to address these burgeoning biosecurity challenges. We aim to foster a dedicated focus on researching, developing, testing, and promoting such 'Biosecurity Safeguards for Generative AI' to ensure responsible innovation at the vital intersection of artificial intelligence and biotechnology.

Objective

By confronting the escalating dual-use risks and vulnerabilities of GenAI in biotechnology, this workshop aims to catalyze a concerted global effort towards embedding robust biosecurity safeguards directly within these powerful tools. Our primary objective is to unite interdisciplinary experts, spanning AI/ML, synthetic biology, bioinformatics, biosecurity policy, and ethics, to forge actionable pathways for the research, development, standardization, and deployment of AI-native safety measures. These include establishing dynamic benchmarks for risk prediction, fostering collaborative red-teaming to uncover vulnerabilities, and engineering built-in technical guardrails encompassing model-level constraints and agent-level defenses. By advancing these critical safeguards, the workshop seeks to ensure that the profound benefits of GenAI in the life sciences are realized responsibly, thereby protecting global health and promoting secure innovation for the benefit of all.

Call for Papers

We invite submissions from researchers working at the intersection of generative AI and biosecurity. Topics of interest include, but are not limited to:

  • Empirical studies of security and safety risks in GenAI systems used for protein design, genome editing, molecular synthesis, and other biotechnological applications.
  • Evaluation methods and benchmarks for stress-testing generative AI models in biological domains.
  • Theoretical foundations for identifying and mitigating risks associated with generative models in the life sciences.
  • Design and implementation of safety mechanisms and protective measures directly integrated into GenAI tools.
  • Analyses of current GenAI applications in biotech that expose safety gaps, with recommendations for improving resilience.

Submission URL: Please submit your work via OpenReview.

Length and Formatting: Submitted papers must be between 4 - 9 pages in PDF format using the NeurIPS 2025 Style Files including figures and tables. Authors are permitted to upload unlimited supplementary materials and references with their submissions. We will use a double-blind review process.
